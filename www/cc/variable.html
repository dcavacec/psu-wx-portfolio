Defined meteorological variables and terms used in this project: 

WEKA:
 Weka contains a collection of visualization tools and algorithms for data analysis and modeling, together with graphical user interfaces.  Weka produces linear regression models that use various combinations of synoptic and hurricane variables to create statistical hurricane track and intensity forecasts. 
Beta effect:
 The beta effect is seen through our analysis. A hurricane rotates cyclonically (anticlockwise) in the northern hemisphere. To the west of the storm, negative (clockwise motion) vorticity develops because f is decreasing as the winds travel southward to lower latitudes. To the east of the storm, positive vorticity develops because f will increase as southerly winds head towards poles (causing a greater shift to the right with increasing latitude). The vorticity imbalance will shift the hurricane towards values of lesser (more negative) vorticity, which is to the west. As the shift in vorticity occurs, the storm becomes misshapen and its center has a nonzero component of wind. This nonzero component of wind will be southerly and give the hurricane a component of northern propagation. The beta effect shifts storms west-north-west of their expected motion which is most strongly dependent on their steering winds. The longitudinal analysis depicts the beta effect correctly. We found that northerly motion will lead to a positive change in longitude, which corresponds to westerly motion. Southerly motion will lead to easterly motion.
Angular momentum:
 Angular momentum is a conserved quantity. Angular momentum is defined by velocity * radius. Angular momentum is useful in analyzing numerous variables in our weka data. It explains why the radius of maximum winds increases with decreasing intensity. This property was most clearly shown when we analyzed intensity, latitude, and landmask variables. The radius of maximum wind speed is typically found at the inner edge of the eyewall. The eye will decrease in size with an increase in intensity to conserve angular momentum. As the velocity (intensity) of a storm increases, the radius must decrease according to the above equation. As a storm travels onto land or into colder waters, its intensity will decrease. The radius of maximum winds will therefore increase because the winds are not blowing as strong and conservation must occur.
Vorticity:
 Absolute vorticity is conserved on Earth. The absolute vorticity consists of the sum of relative vorticity and planetary vorticity. Planetary vorticity (f) increases in the directions of the poles. To compensate and conserve absolute vorticity, as a storm travels to higher latitudes, its relative vorticity must decrease because planetary vorticity increases. Vorticity is du/dx - dv/dy and represents spin in the atmosphere. As a hurricane travels to higher latitudes, all the radii of equal wind contours will increase because the storm is not spun up as tightly since it has less relative vorticity. As the hurricane travels to lower latitudes, the radii will shrink and the storm spins faster and contracts inward. Vorticity arguments are well depicted in our analysis of the radius of 34 knot winds. With a positive change in latitude, the radius of 34 knot winds increases. The opposite is true for a negative change in latitude.
Sheering winds:
 A main component of hurricane movement depends upon the direction of the winds the storm is embedded in. The mean of the wind layers will determine the direction. This is seen in our longitude analysis. If easterly winds are present, the change in longitude will be negative. This negative change represents easterly hurricane movement (the hurricane is moving into more negative longitude positions). If westerly winds are present, the change in longitude will be positive, leading to a positive (westerly) longitude change. The V component of the wind was a smaller influence in the equation. A southerly flow lead to eastern movement while northerly flow leads to western movement for 925mb and 300mb winds. The opposite is true for the 850mb and 700mb layer, which may reflect the beta effect slightly. This argument does not match sheering, but perhaps shows the influence of the coriolis force to direct the storm to the right of the winds.
Storm classifications:
 Tropical depression: < 34 knots
Tropical storm: 34-63 knots  
Category 1 Hurricane: 64-82knots
Category 2 Hurricane: 83-95 knots
Category 3 Hurricane: 96- 112 knots
Category 4 Hurricane: 113-135 knots
Category 5 Hurricane: >135 knots
Radius of Maximum Winds:
 This radius represents the distance from the center of the storm at which maximum wind speed is reached.
Radius of 50 knot winds:
 This is representative of the distance from the center of the storm to wind speeds of 50 knots.
Radius of 34 knot winds :
 This is representative of the distance from the center of the storm to wind speeds of tropical depression strength.
Radius of 64 knot winds:
 This is representative of the distance from the center of the storm to wind speeds of category 1 hurricane strength.
Intensity:
 A measure of the energy of a storm.  In this project, the intensity is most efficiently used as a comparison between storms.
U Wind:
 This is the zonal wind speed.  The zonal wind speed is movement in the east and west directions.  If the zonal wind is positive, the wind flow is from west to east.  If the zonal wind is negative, the wind flow is from east to west. In our model we used the wind from points surrounding the storm in order to try to capture the background environment of the storm. We did not include the wind directly on top of the storm in an attempt to ignore the strong winds produced by the storm itself.
V Wind:
 This is the meridional wind speed.  The meridional wind speed is movement in the north and south directions.  If the meridional wind is positive, the wind flow is from south to north.  If the zonal wind is negative, the wind flow is from north to south.
Shear:
 The wind difference between various pressure levels.  The difference between the 925 and 250mb level is frequently used to predict the future paths and intensities of hurricanes.
Latitude:
 The meridional component of an imaginary grid system breaking Earth into easy to reference coordinates.  One degree latitude is 6076 feet, and there are 90 degrees of latitude in each hemisphere.
Longitude:
 The zonal component of an imaginary grid system breaking Earth into easy to reference coordinates.  A degree of longitude length depends on the circumference of the Earth and thus the degree of latitude.
“Variable” Change Back:
 Averages two variables over a three hour time period.  The variable three hours prior is subtracted from the present variable. 
“Variable” Change Forward:
 Averages two variables over a three hour time period.  The variable three hours in advance is subtracted from the present variable.
Relative Humidity:
 A measure of the amount of moisture in the atmosphere.  The relative humidity is a ratio between the vapor pressure and the equilibrium vapor pressure or the mixing ration and saturation mixing ratio. 
Land/Sea Mask:
 A simple observation of whether the storm was over land.  If over land, a value of 1 was assigned; if over water, a value of 0 was assigned. 
Sea Surface Temperature:
 For our project this refers to the average sea surface temperature beneath and around the storms current position. In cases where the storm is over land, this variable may refer to land surface temperature.
