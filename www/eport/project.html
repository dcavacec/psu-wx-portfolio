Independant Study: Computer Programming
Timeframe Summer - Fall 2009
Knowledge Gained Syntax, development and debugging, and practical advantages of the Perl, Fortran, and VBA Excel programming languages.
Context An investigation to the daily maximum, minimum, and average temperature trend lengths above or below their associated thirty-year normal values in State College, PA since 1896.
Relevance Complex weather derivative valuation based on climotological anomaly frequencies.
Proficiency in designing, coding, and implementing scripts and macros for practical use.

At Penn State's Spring 2008 Meteorology Career Days, I learned from several potential employers that a need for greater programming knowledge in meteorology graduates was becoming evident. With this discovery, I initiated the three-credit independent study option with Dr. George Young, Department of Meteorology professor and Information Technology and Weather Risk Management specialist. Following the suggestions of several industry leaders, we decided to utilize the Perl, Fortran, and VBA Excel programming languages to unveil historical temperature trend-length frequencies in State College.

Perl was chosen for several reasons. Its compatibility with text data was ideal for collecting the historical temperatures because this information was most easily accessed via the PSU weather station page. Using pattern matching, regular expressions, and the browser agent community module 'WWW::Mechanize,' Perl did with ease what other languages would struggle to do. Retrieving all of the needed information was literally a matter of commanding the computer to visit each day's record individually, and record the findings in a text file.

Fortran's extremely fast processing speed and popularity in the scientific research community made it ideal for the processing of the collected data. By designing a simple if-then-else loop to analyze the dataset, a histogram of temperature trend run-length was created and saved again as a text file. After coding was complete, the computation of three histograms describing over 100,000 temperature values took less than thirty seconds.

Finally, Visual Basic for Excel was used to produce graphical output of the test results. Excel's ability to record macros and automatically code the associated script (like a WYSIWYG editor) made learning the VBA language easy. The processes of importing the histogram results, organizing them by temperature type, producing and formatting graphs, and exporting and saving images were all automated with VBA, and could be commenced upon opening the workbook.

The significance of this project included raising my programmatic knowledge of three different coding languages to a functional level in less than two semesters, as well as gaining insight to the way climatological analysis can lead to more accurate valuation of complex weather derivatives. Namely, that historical average, high, and low temperature trend frequencies can be used to estimate the relevant probabilities of such derivative indices as long-term HDD and CDD contracts, or CAT contracts. Price Risk Management: CBOT Commodity Futures
Timeframe Spring 2009
Knowledge Gained Commodity futures sensitivity to weather and other factors, trading strategies, financial forecasting, portfolio management.
Context Assessing and managing the risk around the price of soy requirements for fictional company, Nittany Burger, using CBOT May 2009 soybean futures.
Relevance The process by which weather, among other things, impacts the cost of commodity futures, and how long term planning can protect against adverse prices at the time of purchase.

This project involved using CBOT futures to manage the price risk associated with a purchase of 50,000 bushels of soybeans for a fictional meatless burger company. The ultimate goal was to correctly anticipate market price movement in the long term, and adjust the position to a maximum of 20 contracts net long or short in response to the forecasted change. To attain the purchase, a net long position of 10 contracts had to be assumed by the final day of trading. This required an analysis of outside financial, commodity, energy, and currency markets, seasonal weather forecasts, historical analysis of May 2009 soybean futures price behaviors, and news regarding the fundamental factors affecting soybean economics, such as Argentine farmers' relationship with its government and the global stock of soybeans as the season progressed.

The portfolio management in this project also required daily monitoring of account balances, which were marked-to-market each day, with an initial margin requirement of 4.725% and a maintenance requirement per contract of 3.5% of the total budget. This provided a valuable lesson about the need to carefully manage funds with margin requirements in mind, because a large price swing against net position could spell disaster.

Finally, the length of the project (about three months) provided the trader with insight to the perils of short term trades made in response to daily news developments or changes in price direction. Volatility in the market on a daily or weekly scale is something that should be avoided by taking a position that reflects long term expectations of price development. In this way, fundamental and technical developments in conflict with the trader's expectations can be used as motivation to alter the position if needed. Acting solely as a reaction to such developments is a costly strategy, and successful price management requires anticipation based on history, and outside analysis.

Energy Management: A Corporate Perspective
Timeframe Spring 2009
Knowledge Gained Business modeling, cost/benefit analysis, project valuation, market volatility evaluation, marketing.
Context Comparing the costs of hiring third-party consulting to the expected company benefit, as well as to a 'do nothing approach'. Large retail corporation (10+ sites in multiple states) energy expenditure was the focus. The analysis, results, and competitive advantage was delivered in a consulting news brief.
Relevance Discovering new ways to gain hedging/speculative edges as a result of thrid-party weather risk management consulting services, as well as how to professionally deliver the competitive advantage of such a firm.

A very insightful yet unexpectedly straightforward project, the energy management involved with this corporate finance project involved identifying poor energy expenditures within a large retail corporation, devising a business model for how they can improve, marketing the consulting services to the appropriate party, conducting cost/benefit analyses and market research, and delivering the result. Our group chose Fry's Electronics, a corporation similar to Best Buy what was found to be spending unnecessary amounts on natural gas costs by contacting one of their financial representatives. After determining their locations, estimating total expenditures and potential savings through our services, and delivering a marketing tool similar to a news letter, we moved forward by choosing more cost-efficient sources of natural gas for the company. Furthermore, by evaluating the volatility of the NYMEX natural gas futures, we identified which futures months of natural gas needs would be appropriate to hedge. This project taught business modeling, marketing, cost estimating, and hedging over the course of about three months.
Hedging: NYMEX Natural Gas Futures
Timeframe Six weeksduring Fall of 2008
Knowledge Gained Natural gas futures prices reaction to weather and climatological anomales, the U.S. Dollar, crude oil markets, and commitment of traders reports.
Context Hedging the natural gas requirements of fictional company Happy Valley Chocolates with 800 January 2009 futures contracts and 600 February 2009 and March 2009 futures contracts.
Relevance Learning the methods of hedging purchasing requirements so that its application can be improved with advanced weather analysis.

Using the NYMEX natural gas futures settlement prices quoted on the NYMEX website, contracts were strategically purchased over a six week period in order to ensure delivery of first quarter natural gas requirements at the lowest weighted average proce possible. Monthly requirements were 8 MM BTU's in January, and 6 MM BTU's in February in March, corresponding to a required 800 Januray 2009 and 600 February 2009 and March 2009 futures contracts requirement. Since the task involved purchasing at the best price and did not allow for speculation, short positions were not allowed to be taken over the course of the project. Therefore, identifying the most likely time for minimum contract prices to occur was key to sucess.

Trading of all three or none of the contracts on any given day was allowed, but a minimum of 100 and maximum of 500 contracts per futures month was placed. On any given day, between 100 and 500 contracts could be traded, with increments of 100 contracts. Key sources of information for analysis and forecasting of price direction included Prudential Energy Reports, Bloomberg.com, the Department of Energy website, the U.S. Commodity Futures Trading Commission website, as well as news sources such as Yahoo and Google.

Speculative Trading: ICE Brent Crude Oil Futures
Timeframe Four weeks during Fall of 2008
Knowledge Gained Fundamental factors influencing crude oil futures prices, speculative trading for profit, portfolio management, cooperative trading.
Context Fictional hedge fund Lions Trading allocated $30M in capital to generate a minimum return of 20% per year via speculative trading of ICE Brent crude oil futures.
Relevance The implications of working as a team under trading restrictions to generate profit requirements using fundamental influences and energy reports (Prudential).

The ICE Brent crude oil futures trading project simulated the responsibilities of a speculative trader employed by a large hedge fund. Having been allocated $30M and restricted to making a total of 1,000 long and 1,000 short trades based on the settlement prices quoted daily on the Intercontinental Exchange's website, the team was tasked with generating the largest possible profit by making trades of sizes between 200 and 500 contracts. Increments of 100 contracts were required as well, which means a minimum of 4 and a maximum of 10 trades would be made of the duration of the project. The margin requirement of each contract was $10k, and this prompted traders to consider the pros and cons of each possible net position when making trade decisions. In addition, the calculation of final net gain was based on the average settlement prices of the short and long trades made.

Completing this project within a group setting provided key insights to the implications of having several decision makers work toward the same goal. For example, group rules were established to prevent any discrepancy regarding the timing, magnitude, or general strategy involved with speculation. Each group member was assigned a specific title or position during the exercise as well, and this allowed the ultimate goal to be achieved through independent input as well as collaboration.
