The best longitude predictor was found using the Kiveo dataset. This dataset had fewer variables than other (particularly our dataset). The variables removed from the run were the forward differences for latitude, radius of max winds, intensity, and radius of 34 knot winds. The LandSeaTemp variable was also removed because it slightly decreased the error while not changing the correlation coefficient. The linear regression equation disregarded variables from the dataset in its creation of the equation. The past change in intensity, past change in radius of maximum wind, past change of radius of 34 knot winds, v winds at 700mb, and v winds at 500mb were disregarded in compilation. Attempts were made to consolidate the number of variables, however, when more variables were removed, the correlation coefficient decreased and error increased slightly. The U400 winds and the RH were removed because their coefficients were less than .02. But these variables back into the data during the final analysis.

To analyze the variables with respect to longitude prediction, we start with the past latitude change. We have a positive coefficient. If the storm moves from lower latitudes to higher latitudes, the change in latitude will be positive representing northerly motion. (In differencing formulas, the difference is found by subtracting the past value from the present value). If the storm moves from higher latitudes to lower latitudes, the change is latitude will be negative, representing southerly motion. Northerly motion will lead to a positive change in longitude, which corresponds to easterly motion (less negative value minus more negative value because we have our longitude set up as more negative further west). Southerly motion will lead to westerly motion.

The u wind motion coefficients are mainly positive in this linear regression equation. The only negative wind coefficient value is at 850. Hurricane movement and direction is most influenced by the direction of the winds (I took 452 with Dr. Frank!). If easterly winds are present, the change in longitude will be negative. This negative change represents westerly hurricane movement (the hurricane is moving into more negative longitude positions). If westerly winds are present, the change in longitude will be positive, leading to a positive (easterly) longitude change.

The v wind component is split in coefficient sign. V wind of 925mb and 300mb agree with the above argument of hurricane motion most influenced by the winds. If southerly winds are present at 925mb and 300mb, the change in longitude will be positive, leading to hurricane movement towards the east. If northerly winds are present at 925mb and 300mb, hurricane movement is toward west. A southerly V wind at 850mb and 400mb will lead to hurricane movement to the west. A northerly wind at 850mb and 400mb will lead to hurricane movement to the east.

The relative humidity is always going to be positive, and since the coefficient is positive, it will cause an eastward (positive) change in longitude. This would only make sense if the u motion is mainly westerly. This is because a continually positive relative humidity value will result in a positive longitude change which corresponds to eastward storm movement.

The radius of maximum winds is, from the National Hurricane Center, the distance from the center of a tropical cyclone to the location of the cyclone's maximum winds. In well-developed hurricanes, the radius of maximum winds is generally found at the inner edge of the eyewall.

For the radius of maximum winds, we (with the help of classmates) determined our dataset to provide the best correlation coefficient. The correlation coefficient was low nonetheless, maxing out at .4383 with a relative absolute error of 90.7137%. In attempts to improve these values I discarded as many unimportant variables as possible. I discarded all the forward change variables besides the radius of maximum wind forward change and sea surface temperature forward change. The second run, I discarded longitude, sst, uwind at 250mb, uwind at 700mb, vwind at 250mb, because the coefficient variables were small. After group discussion, on the next run I took out all of the u and v wind values because we related the radius of maximum wind speed to intensity. From previous experiment, we determined that the intensity of the storm was not dependent on the winds. The radius of maximum wind speed has more to do with the strength of the storm rather than the wind values. This slightly increased the correlation coefficient and decreased the error. I tried to discard the land/sea mask but it decreased the correlation coefficient and increased the error. Interestingly, the equation does not use the past radius of maximum wind change or the past or future sea surface temperature change.

The latitude has a positive coefficient, meaning that at higher latitudes the radius of maximum winds will increase. The radius of maximum winds is often found at the edge of the eyewall. This is tricky variable to analyze because hurricane eye size is not always a direct measure of hurricane strength and intensity. At higher latitudes, the intensity of hurricanes should decrease because there is less energy available in colder waters to provide latent heat to power the hurricane. Our data holds true for some hurricanes, because the stronger the storm, the smaller the eye. As these storms weaken at higher latitudes, the eye decompresses and increases in size.

The intensity variable has a negative coefficient value. Intensity will always be positive, meaning this variable will contribute to a negative change in radius of maximum winds. As the intensity increases, the radius of maximum winds decreases. This agrees with the above thought of stronger storms having smaller and more compact eyes.

The coefficient for the radius of maximum winds is negative. The radius of maximum wind will always be positive, meaning that it will make the radius of maximum wind speed (RMW) change negative. The larger the RMW, the more the RMW will decrease. This make sense because a large radius of maximum wind will want to intensify and become smaller and more condensed.

The change in longitude has a positive coefficient. If the longitude change is positive, the storm is movement is to the east. This corresponds to a positive RMW change. If the longitude change is negative, the storm movement is to the west. This corresponds to a negative RMW change. In the Atlantic, as storms move further into the Atlantic the temperature generally increases slightly. The temperature certainly increases once entering the Gulf Stream territory. This map shows the sea surface temperature gradient of the Atlantic Ocean for April 17th, 2008 (the Gulf Stream is well defined):
http://www.erh.noaa.gov/box/maps/sst/2008041700_sst_AB.png
Once again, assuming temperature gradient increasing to the west, this agrees with warmer water leading to smaller values of the RMW.

The change in intensity has a negative coefficient. If the intensity change is positive, the RMW will be negative and show a decrease. If the intensity change is negative, the RMW will be positive and show an increase. This again agrees with a smaller eye for stronger storms.

The land/sea mask has a positive coefficient. This variable is zero if over sea, meaning that it does not contribute to a change in RMW. The contribution becomes large if the storm is over land. As the storm travels over land, the RMW will increase, meaning that the storm will weaken.

Overall a .9468 correlation coefficient with a .06 mean absolute error showed a very good result for predicting latitude change using our Cane Coder's dataset. When looking at past changes in latitude and longitude, the past (latchangeback and lonchangeback) should positive because when looking at the storm direction change as it progresses; it should be moving north and east, and thus should have a positive increase in values.

While looking at latitude, there are a number of different predictors to consider for the best linear regression model:

Latchangeback had a positive coefficient which means that if in the past 3 hours the storm moved north, it will likely continue to nead north the next 3 hours.

Lonchangeback is also positive. This means that if the storm moved east in the past 3 hours then it will also tend to have a northerly component to its motion. This makes sense becasue storms that move east usually have more of northerly component than westerly moving storms.

The u and v winds are very important to analyze when predicting latitude change in hurricane. While trying to analyze the u and v wind directions to make the correlation coefficient even higher for latitude; we tried averaging the u and v winds for both upper level (250-500) and lower level (700-925). When using the average u winds, both the upper level and lower level average winds had negative coefficients. The upper level u winds proved to be more important than the lower levels. The v average upper and lower level winds were positive, and thus these correlate to the latitude changing to a northwesterly direction as predicted. The beta effect can help explain this because when storms are embedded in an easterly flow, which the negative u winds show, storms at low latitudes tend to shift northwesterly; thus the positive v winds. We also averaged the u and v winds from 500-850, because we felt at this level winds are most important when predicting hurricane track movement. When using only the u and v average winds between 500 and 850mb, the correlation coefficient went down slightly. This is most likely due to a lesser input of statistical factors into the equation. However the u and v predicted directions still make sense for latitude change; u change negative means an easterly wind which means an easterly flow for the beta effect would cause a northwesterly wind and thus a positive v wind.

The sea surface temperature change back is positive meaning that if the storm is moving into warmer waters it has a tendacy to have more of a northerly component to its direction.

Max radius, which corresponds with the strongest winds, has a positive coefficient which means as the radius increases the storm tends to move more north. This makes sense becasue when the radius increases the storm is usually weakening and weaker storms tend to move more north than stronger storms.. Although this proved not to be an important factor in determining change in latitude, because when taken out the correlation coefficient does not change.

Land/sea did not have a major effect on the latitude change, because it will be over sea for most of the time and thus have a 0 value that we assigned to sea.

The above is the simple linear regression output for a 3 hour intensity change forecast. It had a correlation of .7264. Other methods were tried, but the linear regression still had the best correlation. Below is a description of each variable used and a brief physical description of why the coefficient makes sense.

Latitude has negative coefficient. This means that the further north a storm, the smaller its intensity change will be. This makes sense because storms tend to intensify less the further north they are.

Longitude has a slight positive coefficient. This means that storms tend to increase in intensity more when they are further east. This makes some physical sense, because most storms go through an initial intensification after they form in the east Atlantic, while storms further west may either weaken or strengthen.

Intensity has a negative coefficient. This means that the stronger a storm currently is the less it will intensify in 3 hours. This makes sense because storms can only become so strong before they start to weaken at least a little.

Intensity change previous has a strong positive correlation to intensity change future. This makes sense because if a storm intensified the last 3 hours, then it will most likely intensify the next 3 hours. The coefficient of .7 means that the previous intensity change is minimized a little in the model, which also makes sense.

SST is another important variable in predicting 3 hour intensity changes. It has a positive coefficient meaning the warmer the water the larger the increase in intensity. This agrees with the current understanding of hurricanes.

Land/sea has a value of 0 if the storm is over water, and 1 if over land. It has a negative coefficient, meaning that a storm over land will weaken faster than a storm over water. This is also what is understood and observed currently.

I also ran multiple model runs, using only one variable to predict future intensity change. The best variable was by far the past intensity change, which is expected since this is a statistical model. The second best was the change in the radius of maximum winds. This variable had a low correlation, but had a negative coefficient. This makes sense, because the smaller the eye of a hurricane becomes, usually the stronger it will become. What is interesting is that this variable was not used by the computer when it was allowed to choose from multiple variables. This must mean that when paired with the group of variables above, the max radius change is not significant. Our forecast is only for 3 hours, so for longer time periods it may be. Other variables that the computer did not find significant where the u and v wind shear and the absolute values of those shears. This may be because a current wind shear may take longer than 3 hours to impact a storm. If we have made a variable that calculated the change in wind shear that may have been more useful, but once again our timescale may have been too short.
Sea Surface Temperature Model:

Overall there was a correlation coefficient of .8261 and an error of 1.69 (higher error) in predicting sea surface temperatures, but this is an important variable to consider when predicting hurricane tracks. Higher sea surface temperatures can be a key indicator in hurricane intensification. For sea surface temperatures, the past sea surface temperatures should be warmer than the predicted temperatures. This may seem contradictory, but this makes sense because as latitude increases as you go further north, sea surface temperatures should be lower. The u and v wind directions change sporadically throughout the lower to upper levels and are secondary to the latchangeback variable. Therefore they are not important synoptically for the latitude change analysis.

When looking at sea surface temperature, this was the best linear regression model we could come up with, using Cane Coder's dataset.

A negative coefficient for latitude means the further north the storm track is, the colder the sea surface temperature; which makes sense.

Longitude also has a negative coefficient which means the further east the storm is, the colder the sea surface temperature which makes sense.

Intensity should increase as sea surface temperature increases, and this also proved true.

Lonchangeback has a negative coefficient, which means a higher sea surface temperature as the storm moves west.

A positive maxradchange back coefficient means as the radius increases, the higher the sea surface temperature.

Winds do not play a major factor in predicting sea surface temperature as the coefficients for these were all small, and there is no meteorological physical explanation for this.

Land/sea should only be important over the sea when considering sea surface temperatures, and thus this is not a major predictor either.
